Why Data Analysis & Visualisation?

This competency sits at the core of what Phase 2 of Project RESOLVE demanded: translating raw EVM figures, resource data, and risk indicators into analytical narratives that a project board could act on immediately. Without data literacy, a CPI of 0.82 is just a number. With it, you can trace that number back to a TQ approval bottleneck, quantify its cost, and recommend a specific intervention. This artefact demonstrates that chain — from raw data through analysis to decision — across four interconnected analytical layers.

Earned Value Management — Cost & Schedule Analysis

Analytical layer 1: translating three data points into a performance trajectory visible to a non-technical project board.

Figure 1 · EVM Performance Chart — PV, EV & AC plotted Month 0–12 with variance zones · Project RESOLVE
Decision Logic & Analytical Judgement

The key analytical decision here was not technical — it was communicative. A table showing PV = £32m, EV = £28m, AC = £34m tells a project board three numbers. The line chart with shaded variance zones shows them a trajectory: the EV line falls below PV from Month 3, not Month 12 — meaning underperformance was structurally embedded from the earliest mobilisation phase, not caused by the Month 12 supplier failure. That distinction changes the diagnosis entirely. The shaded areas convert abstract indices (SPI, CPI) into physically visible gaps a board member can grasp without project management training.

Decision Logic & Analytical Judgement

The discipline of building this dashboard was to refuse to stop at the symptom. CPI = 0.82 is a symptom. The cause is that contractors are standing idle at day rates because TQs take five days to clear — and that delay is itself caused by an under-resourced engineering team. Identifying that causal chain required cross-referencing the EVM data with the MS Project Resource Work Summary. The dashboard is only valuable because it shows the chain, not just the headline.

Professional Judgement Note

The 5/5 rating assigned to the tier-1 supplier failure is a professional analytical decision, not a mechanical output. It required interpreting the EVM evidence — a −£6m cost variance and 8-week critical path delay already realised — alongside an assessment of the project's remaining recovery capacity. A risk that has already produced these impacts at Month 12 of a 36-month project, with no secondary supplier ready, warrants the highest possible rating. The evidence column makes that reasoning transparent and auditable.

Decision Logic & Analytical Judgement

The Resource Work Summary (Figure 3) makes the engineering bottleneck visible instantly: contractor utilisation towers over internal engineering capacity. This single visual explained why TQ resolution exceeded five days and why EV generation stalled. Cross-referencing it with the Gantt chart (Figures 4 & 5) confirmed that the 8-week supplier delay hit predecessor activities on the critical path — not tasks with float — meaning the schedule variance cannot be absorbed without deliberate compression action. These four data sources, read together rather than in isolation, produce the systemic diagnosis that drives the recommendations below.
